A voice for informed emigration
June 2019

I've emigrated from Poland when I was 24 years old to join the PhD program in southern Germany. During the goodbye party, my friends told me I wouldn't survive it over there. First, what threatened me was starvation since I'm a vegetarian. Second, I'd probably remain sober cause I don't like beer and lonely - since I will be fighting people about the second world war. Those predictions didn't pan out, but likewise, didn't my stereotypes, both positive and negative.

Germans turned out to be quite lovely and friendly. Every german restaurant has vegetarian options, southwest Germany turned out to be a wine-producing region, and my German peers would often ask about the history first. In general, it seemed to me that Germans are active, political, open-minded, and pragmatical, not attached to the hierarchy, titles, and status symbols. Old germans from the neighbouring villages would ask us why is Poland so conservative and anti-EU lately. On the other hand, dentists and gynaecologists would switch from fluent English to german since we are in Germany. The university wouldn't allow for any flexible teaching format. I still have nightmares of fighting with German bureaucracy to get married (8 weeks to get a date).

Emigration forces a lifestyle change, and there is no point fighting it. Conscious emigration will challenge ignorance. It turned out I knew nothing, not even about the history of far-away places, but not even a bit of a society of the closest neighbouring country. In my case, emigration is never-as-expected, although preparations. I found it easier to fit-in in the small international Tuebingen, than in the privileged Warsaw bubble. Now, with every trip home, I find my peers there more and more conservative. Hard to understand who's changing more, me or them.

I've moved from a german informatics department to an Italian university hospital - let's see how it goes.
